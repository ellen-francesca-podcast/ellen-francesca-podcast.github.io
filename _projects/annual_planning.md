This template is split into three parts: **Review**, **Reflect**, and **Plan**. It's based on how I plan my own year, as well as some books, podcasts & videos I've found helpful over the years. 

Even if you don't use this exact template, I recommend structuring your annual review into these three sections: 

1.  Review the last year 
2.  Reflect on the remainder of your PhD
3.  Plan for the new year

Skipping one often leaves me with less clarity about my goals or worse leads me to not acting on them (since I didn't define actionable next steps). That said, feel free to take whatever feels useful to you and leave what doesn't. This template is simply meant to help you approach your PhD more proactively :) 

I'd love to hear any thoughts, feedback or recommendations you have!

### How would an ideal PhD look like for you? ‚ú®

Define your ideal vs good-enough PhD. Try to be as specific as possible. 

**Reflection Prompts (Optional)**
- *What would an **ideal average week** in your PhD look like?*
- *What are your ideal contributions (research outputs, projects, experiments, papers, software)?*
- *What skills do you want to graduate with?*
- *Which conferences would you like to attend? What talks or presentations would you like to give?*
- *What does a **good-enough PhD** look like for you?*
- *What is your minimum viable thesis?*

Consider research output, skills, work-life balance, and your ambitions/goals. Defining your own "good enough" helps you set realistic expectations and prevents perfectionism from derailing progress.

### Learning & skills üß†
You might, like me, find it difficult to schedule learning goals on a monthly basis. I recommend planning them quarterly instead, and identifying interesting resources, potential risks, and concrete actions for each skill and topic in advance. Often you need to sign up for training courses months in advance. Learning and skill development can easily feel less urgent than immediate research tasks, which makes it easy to neglect them. However, consistently making time to acquire new skills and read will ultimately make you a better researcher and I would argue should be an essential part of your PhD.


**Prompts:**
- *Which skills are essential to complete your research or advance your career this year?*
- *What topics would you like to explore just out of pure curiosity?*
- *What resources (books, courses, mentors, workshops) will help you develop these skills?*
- *What specific actions will ensure progress (e.g., schedule reading sessions, sign up for a summer school )?*
